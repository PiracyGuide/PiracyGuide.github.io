So while your file is downloading, let's learn about what all those words mean, and how torrents work.  To put it simply, there are many computers all over the internet that have the file you are trying to download.  Instead of downloading from a single centralized website, your torrent client is finding all of those people that are sharing that file, and it's downloading from all of them at the same time.  If you are downloading a video, it might download the first part from one person, the second part from a different person, and yet another part from yet another person.

Of course theres a bit more to it than that, but there are a few benefits to that.  The first one is download speed.  Asumming that a torrent has a lot of seeders. (The people you're downloading from.) you can get a very high download speed. There are a lot of computers sending this file to you at the same time, instead of just one.  Another benefit is that the download will survive much longer.  Because there are so many people to download it from, if a server goes offline or the file is accidentally deleted from somewhere, theres still many other places it can be downloaded from.

So to get back on track, let's talk about how to seeder and peer system works.  Right now, you are likely downloading a torrent.  This means you are what's called a peer.  A peer is every person interacting with a certain torrent.  There are two types of peers.  The first type is called a leecher.  A leecher is anyone who is currently downloading a torrent. After your torrent is completely done downloading, you'll notice that your torrent client will say "Seeding".  That means you've become the second type of peer.  a seeder.  A seeder is anyone who has completely downloaded the torrent, and is now sharing the file to others.

Torrents are peer-to-peer. This means it's kind of a community effort.  You download the file from all of these people, then once you have the file, other people download it from you. This way everyone helps everyone.

I've made a small demonstration below of this process, if that helps your understanding.

 

Most torrent clients track what's called a ratio.  This is a ....ratio... that compare how much you have downloaded from other people, versus how much you have uploaded to others.  It is common curtesy to at least give back an equal amount.  Meaning that if you download a 4.56 gigbyte movie, seed the file until other people have downloaded at least 4.56 gigbytes from you. This would be a ratio of 1.    If you download a 10gb game, and you upload 25gbs back to the community, that would be a ratio of 2.5.

 

I keep talking about how you're downloading from other people, and uploading to other people.  But how does your client know which people have this file to download from?  That's where trackers come in.  A tracker is a website/service that keeps "track" of who has what.
